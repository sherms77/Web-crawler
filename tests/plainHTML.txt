<p>A <b>soft drink</b> (see <a href="#Terminology">§ Terminology</a> for other names) is a <a href="/wiki/Drink" title="Drink">drink</a> that usually contains water (often <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Sweetness" title="Sweetness">sweetener</a>, and a natural and/or <a href="/wiki/Artificial_Flavoring" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial Flavoring">artificial flavoring</a>. The sweetener may be a <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/High-fructose_corn_syrup" title="High-fructose corn syrup">high-fructose corn syrup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit_juice" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit juice">fruit juice</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Sugar_substitute" title="Sugar substitute">sugar substitute</a> (in the case of <a href="/wiki/Diet_drink" title="Diet drink"><i>diet drinks</i></a>), or some combination of these. Soft drinks may also contain <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_coloring" title="Food coloring">colorings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservatives</a>, and/or other ingredients.
</p><p>Soft drinks are called "soft" in contrast with "<a href="/wiki/Hard_drink" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard drink">hard</a>" <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_drink" title="Alcoholic drink">alcoholic drinks</a>. Small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a> may be present in a soft drink, but the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol content</a> must be less than 0.5% of the total volume of the drink in many countries and localities<sup id="cite_ref-ECFE_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ECFE-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-alcoholfree.co.uk_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alcoholfree.co.uk-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> if the drink is to be considered <a href="/wiki/Non-alcoholic_drink" title="Non-alcoholic drink">non-alcoholic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fruit_punch" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit punch">Fruit punch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> (even <a href="/wiki/Kombucha" title="Kombucha">kombucha</a>), and other such non-alcoholic drinks are technically soft drinks by this definition, but are not generally referred to as such.
</p><p>Soft drinks may be served cold, over <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> cubes, or at room <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. They are available in many container formats, including <a href="/wiki/Soda_can" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda can">cans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass_bottles" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass bottles">glass bottles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plastic_bottles" class="mw-redirect" title="Plastic bottles">plastic bottles</a>. Containers come in a variety of sizes, ranging from small bottles to large multi-liter containers. Soft drinks are widely available at <a href="/wiki/Fast_food_restaurant" title="Fast food restaurant">fast food restaurants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">movie theaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convenience_store" title="Convenience store">convenience stores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casual-dining_restaurants" class="mw-redirect" title="Casual-dining restaurants">casual-dining restaurants</a>, dedicated <a href="/wiki/Soda_store" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda store">soda stores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vending_machine" title="Vending machine">vending machines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">bars</a> from <a href="/wiki/Soda_fountain" title="Soda fountain">soda fountain</a> machines. Soft drinks are usually served in <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Disposable_cup" title="Disposable cup">disposable cups</a> in the first three venues. In casual dining restaurants and bars, soft drinks are often served in <a href="/wiki/List_of_glassware" title="List of glassware">glasses</a> made from glass or plastic. Soft drinks may be drunk with straws or sipped directly from the cups.
</p><p>Soft drinks are mixed with other ingredients in several contexts. In Western countries, in bars and other places where alcohol is served (e.g. airplanes, restaurants and nightclubs), many <a href="/wiki/Mixed_drink" title="Mixed drink">mixed drinks</a> are made by blending a soft drink with hard liquor and serving the drink over ice. One well-known example is the <a href="/wiki/Rum_and_coke" class="mw-redirect" title="Rum and coke">rum and coke</a>, which may also contain <a href="/wiki/Lime_juice" class="mw-redirect" title="Lime juice">lime juice</a>. Some homemade <a href="/wiki/Fruit_punch" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit punch">fruit punch</a> recipes, which may or may not contain alcohol, contain a mixture of various <a href="/wiki/Fruit_juice" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit juice">fruit juices</a> and a soft drink (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ginger_ale" title="Ginger ale">ginger ale</a>). At <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream_parlor" title="Ice cream parlor">ice cream parlors</a> and 1950s-themed <a href="/wiki/Diner" title="Diner">diners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream_float" title="Ice cream float">ice cream floats</a> consisting of a soft drink poured over <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Root_beer_floats" class="mw-redirect" title="Root beer floats">root beer floats</a>, are often sold. Some types of soft drinks are <a href="/wiki/Lemon-lime_drink" title="Lemon-lime drink">lemon-lime drinks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_soda" class="mw-redirect" title="Orange soda">orange soda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cola" title="Cola">cola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grape_soda" class="mw-redirect" title="Grape soda">grape soda</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Root_beer" title="Root beer">root beer</a>.
</p><p>Within a decade of the invention of carbonated water by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> in 1767 inventors in Britain and in Europe had used his concept to produce the drink in greater quantities, with one such inventor, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Jacob_Schweppe" title="Johann Jacob Schweppe">J. J. Schweppe</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Schweppes" title="Schweppes">Schweppes</a> in 1783 and selling the world's first bottled soft drink.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Atlantic_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Soft drink brands introduced in the 19th century include <a href="/wiki/R._White%27s_Lemonade" title="R. White&#39;s Lemonade">R. White's Lemonade</a> in 1845, <a href="/wiki/Dr_Pepper" title="Dr Pepper">Dr Pepper</a> in 1885, and <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> in 1886. Subsequent brands include <a href="/wiki/Pepsi" title="Pepsi">Pepsi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irn-Bru" title="Irn-Bru">Irn-Bru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sprite_(drink)" title="Sprite (drink)">Sprite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fanta" title="Fanta">Fanta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/7_UP" class="mw-redirect" title="7 UP">7 UP</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Names_for_soft_drinks_in_the_United_States" title="Names for soft drinks in the United States">Names for soft drinks in the United States</a></div>
<p>While the term "soft drink" is commonly used in product labeling and on restaurant menus, in many countries these drinks are more commonly referred to by regional names, including <i>carbonated drink</i>, <i>cool drink</i>, <i>cold drink</i>, <i>fizzy drink</i>, <i>fizzy juice</i>, <i>lolly water</i>, <i>pop</i>, <i>seltzer</i>, <i>soda</i>, <i>coke</i>, <i>soda pop</i>, <i>tonic</i>, and <i>mineral</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-dialect_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dialect-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-irish_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irish-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Due to the high sugar content in typical soft drinks, they may also be called <i>sugary drinks</i>.
</p><p>In the United States, the 2003 <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a> Dialect Survey tracked the usage of the nine most common names. Over half of the survey respondents preferred the term "soda", which was dominant in the Northeastern United States, California, and the areas surrounding Milwaukee and St. Louis. The term "pop", which was preferred by 25% of the respondents, was most popular in the Midwest and Pacific Northwest, while the <a href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" class="mw-redirect" title="Genericized trademark">genericized trademark</a> "coke", used by 12% of the respondents, was most popular in the Southern United States.<sup id="cite_ref-dialect_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dialect-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The term "tonic" is distinctive to eastern <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, although usage is declining.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the English-speaking parts of Canada, the term "pop" is prevalent, but "soft drink" is the most common English term used in Montreal.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom and Ireland, the term "fizzy drink" is common. "Pop" and "fizzy pop" are used in Northern England, South Wales, and the Midlands, while<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> "mineral"<sup id="cite_ref-irish_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irish-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> or "lemonade" (as a general term) are used in Ireland. In Scotland, "fizzy juice" or even simply "juice" is colloquially encountered. In Australia and New Zealand, "soft drink"<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> or "fizzy drink" is typically used.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/South_African_English" title="South African English">South African English</a>, "cool drink" is any soft drink.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In other languages, various names are used: descriptive names as "non-alcoholic beverages", equivalents of "soda water", or generalized prototypical names. For example, the Bohemian variant of the Czech language (but not Moravian dialects) uses "limonáda" for all such beverages, not only for those from lemons.  Similarly, the Slovak language uses "malinovka" (= "raspberry water") for all such beverages, not only for raspberry ones.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of soft drinks lie in the development of fruit-flavored drinks. In the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">medieval Middle East</a>, a variety of fruit-flavored soft drinks were widely drunk, such as <a href="/wiki/Sharbat" title="Sharbat">sharbat</a>, and were often sweetened with ingredients such as <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">syrup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>. Other common ingredients included <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamarind" title="Tamarind">tamarind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jujube" title="Jujube">jujube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumac" title="Sumac">sumac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musk" title="Musk">musk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mentha" title="Mentha">mint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>. Middle Eastern drinks later became <a href="/wiki/Islamic_contributions_to_Medieval_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic contributions to Medieval Europe">popular in medieval Europe</a>, where the word "syrup" was derived from <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Tudor_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor England">Tudor England</a>, 'water imperial' was widely drunk; it was a sweetened drink with lemon flavor and containing <a href="/wiki/Cream_of_tartar" class="mw-redirect" title="Cream of tartar">cream of tartar</a>. 'Manays Cryste' was a
sweetened cordial flavored with rosewater, violets or cinnamon.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another early type of soft drink was <a href="/wiki/Lemonade" title="Lemonade">lemonade</a>, made of water and lemon juice sweetened with honey, but without carbonated water. The <i>Compagnie des Limonadiers</i> of Paris was granted a monopoly for the sale of lemonade soft drinks in 1676. Vendors carried tanks of lemonade on their backs and dispensed cups of the soft drink to Parisians.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonated_drinks">Carbonated drinks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carbonated drinks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg/300px-Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg/450px-Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg/600px-Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bubbles of carbon dioxide float to the surface of a <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated</a> soft drink.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbonated drinks</b> or <b>fizzy drinks</b> are beverages that contain dissolved <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolution</a> of CO<sub>2</sub> in a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>, gives rise to <i>fizz</i> or <i>effervescence</i>.   The process usually involves carbon dioxide under high pressure. When the pressure is removed, the carbon dioxide is released from the solution as small bubbles, which causes the solution to become <a href="/wiki/Effervescence" title="Effervescence">effervescent</a>, or fizzy. A common example is the dissolving of carbon dioxide in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a>. Carbon dioxide is only weakly soluble in water, therefore it separates into a <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> when the pressure is released.
</p><p>Carbonated beverages are prepared by mixing the flavored syrup with carbonated water, both chilled.  Carbonation levels range up to 5 volumes of CO<sub>2</sub> per liquid volume.  Ginger ale, colas, and related drinks are carbonated with 3.5 volumes.  Other drinks, often fruity ones, are carbonated less.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Engraving of assorted scientific equipment, such as a pneumatic trough. A dead mouse rests under one glass canister." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/220px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/330px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/440px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1063" data-file-height="875" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Equipment used by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> in his experiments on gases and the carbonation of water</div></div></div>
<p>In the late 18th century, scientists made important progress in replicating naturally carbonated <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral waters</a>. In 1767, Englishman <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> first discovered a method of infusing water with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> when he suspended a bowl of distilled water above a beer vat at a local brewery in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, England. His invention of carbonated water (also known as soda water) is the major and defining component of most soft drinks.<sup id="cite_ref-truetex.com_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-truetex.com-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Priestley found that water treated in this manner had a pleasant taste, and he offered it to his friends as a refreshing drink. In 1772, Priestley published a paper entitled <i>Impregnating Water with Fixed Air</i> in which he describes dripping <i>oil of vitriol</i> (or <a href="/wiki/Sulphuric_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulphuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> as it is now called) onto <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> to produce carbon dioxide gas, and encouraging the gas to dissolve into an agitated bowl of water.<sup id="cite_ref-truetex.com_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-truetex.com-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1043612015">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:.5em 1.4em .8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:.5em 0 .8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{overflow:hidden;position:relative;margin:.5em auto .8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft span,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright span{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox>blockquote{margin:0;padding:0;border-left:0;font-family:inherit;font-size:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:110%;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote>:first-child{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote:last-child>:last-child{margin-bottom:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quote-title,.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quotebox-quote{display:block}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:640px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{width:100%!important;margin:0 0 .8em!important;float:none!important}}</style><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:29%; ;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>"Within a decade, inventors in Britain and in Europe had taken Priestley's basic idea—get some "fixed air," mix it with water, shake—and created contraptions that could make carbonated water more quickly, in greater quantities.
One of those inventors was named Johann Jacob Schweppe, who sold bottled soda water and whose business is still around today."
</p>
</blockquote>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">—The Great Soda-Water Shake Up, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i>, October 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Atlantic_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Another Englishman, <a href="/wiki/John_Mervin_Nooth" title="John Mervin Nooth">John Mervin Nooth</a>, improved Priestley's design and sold his apparatus for commercial use in pharmacies. Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Torbern_Bergman" title="Torbern Bergman">Torbern Bergman</a> invented a generating apparatus that made carbonated water from chalk by the use of sulfuric acid. Bergman's apparatus allowed imitation mineral water to be produced in large amounts. Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jacob Berzelius">Jöns Jacob Berzelius</a> started to add flavors (spices, juices, and wine) to carbonated water in the late eighteenth century. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_(apothecary)" title="Thomas Henry (apothecary)">Thomas Henry</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Apothecary" title="Apothecary">apothecary</a> from Manchester, was the first to sell artificial mineral water to the general public for medicinal purposes, beginning in the 1770s. His recipe for 'Bewley's Mephitic Julep' consisted of 3 <a href="/wiki/Dram_(unit)" title="Dram (unit)">drachms</a> of fossil alkali to a <a href="/wiki/Quart" title="Quart">quart</a> of water, and the manufacture had to 'throw in streams of fixed air until all the alkaline taste is destroyed'.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Jacob_Schweppe" title="Johann Jacob Schweppe">Johann Jacob Schweppe</a> developed a process to manufacture bottled <a href="/wiki/Carbonated" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonated">carbonated</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Atlantic_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlantic-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He founded the <a href="/wiki/Schweppes" title="Schweppes">Schweppes</a> Company in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> in 1783 to sell carbonated water,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and relocated his business to London in 1792. His drink soon gained in popularity; among his new found patrons was <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Darwin" title="Erasmus Darwin">Erasmus Darwin</a>. In 1843, the Schweppes company commercialized <a href="/wiki/Malvern_Water" class="mw-redirect" title="Malvern Water">Malvern Water</a> at the Holywell Spring in the Malvern Hills, and received a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Royal_Warrant_holders_of_the_British_royal_family" title="List of Royal Warrant holders of the British royal family">Royal Warrant</a> from King William IV.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was not long before flavoring was combined with carbonated water. The earliest reference to carbonated <a href="/wiki/Ginger_beer" title="Ginger beer">ginger beer</a> is in a <i>Practical Treatise on Brewing</i>. published in 1809. The drinking of either natural or artificial mineral <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> was considered at the time to be a healthy practice, and was promoted by advocates of <a href="/wiki/Temperance_movement" title="Temperance movement">temperance</a>. Pharmacists selling mineral waters began to add herbs and chemicals to unflavored mineral water. They used birch bark (see <a href="/wiki/Birch_beer" title="Birch beer">birch beer</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dandelion">dandelion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smilax_regelii" class="mw-redirect" title="Smilax regelii">sarsaparilla</a>, fruit extracts, and other substances.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_market_and_industrialization">Mass market and industrialization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mass market and industrialization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:(1883)_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg/220px-%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg/330px-%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg/440px-%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg 2x" data-file-width="932" data-file-height="935" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:(1883)_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An 1883 advertisement for <a href="/wiki/Schweppes" title="Schweppes">Schweppes</a> Mineral-Waters</div></div></div>
<p>Soft drinks soon outgrew their origins in the medical world and became a widely consumed product, available cheaply for the masses. By the 1840s, there were more than fifty soft drink manufacturers in London, an increase from just ten in the 1820s.<sup id="cite_ref-History_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Carbonated <a href="/wiki/Lemonade" title="Lemonade">lemonade</a> was widely available in British refreshment stalls in 1833,<sup id="cite_ref-History_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and in 1845, <a href="/wiki/R._White%27s_Lemonade" title="R. White&#39;s Lemonade">R. White's Lemonade</a> went on sale in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> For the <a href="/wiki/Great_Exhibition" title="Great Exhibition">Great Exhibition</a> of 1851 held at <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park</a> in London, Schweppes was designated the official drink supplier and sold over a million bottles of lemonade, ginger beer, <a href="/wiki/Seltzer_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Seltzer water">Seltzer water</a> and soda-water.<sup id="cite_ref-History_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> There was a Schweppes soda water fountain, situated directly at the entrance to the exhibition.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mixer drinks became popular in the second half of the century. <a href="/wiki/Tonic_water" title="Tonic water">Tonic water</a> was originally <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a> added to water as a <a href="/wiki/Prophylaxis" class="mw-redirect" title="Prophylaxis">prophylactic</a> against <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> and was consumed by British officials stationed in the tropical areas of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and Africa. As the quinine powder was so bitter people began mixing the powder with soda and sugar, and a basic tonic water was created. The first commercial tonic water was produced in 1858.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mixed_drink" title="Mixed drink">mixed drink</a> <a href="/wiki/Gin_and_tonic" title="Gin and tonic">gin and tonic</a> also originated in <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British colonial India</a>, when the British population would mix their medicinal quinine tonic with <a href="/wiki/Gin" title="Gin">gin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Image-Codd_bottle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Image-Codd_bottle.jpg/170px-Image-Codd_bottle.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Image-Codd_bottle.jpg/255px-Image-Codd_bottle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Image-Codd_bottle.jpg/340px-Image-Codd_bottle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="460" data-file-height="570" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Image-Codd_bottle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Codd-neck_bottle" title="Codd-neck bottle">Codd-neck bottle</a> invented in 1872 provided an effective seal, preventing the soft drinks from going 'flat'</div></div></div>
<p>A persistent problem in the soft drinks industry was the lack of an effective sealing of the bottles. Carbonated drink <a href="/wiki/Bottle" title="Bottle">bottles</a> are under great pressure from the gas, so inventors tried to find the best way to prevent the carbon dioxide or bubbles from escaping. The bottles could also explode if the pressure was too great. <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Codd" title="Hiram Codd">Hiram Codd</a> devised a patented <a href="/wiki/Bottling" class="mw-redirect" title="Bottling">bottling</a> machine while working at a small mineral water works in the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Road_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Caledonian Road (London)">Caledonian Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islington" title="Islington">Islington</a>, in London in 1870. His <a href="/wiki/Codd-neck_bottle" title="Codd-neck bottle">Codd-neck bottle</a> was designed to enclose a <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a> <a href="/wiki/Washer_(mechanical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Washer (mechanical)">washer</a> in the neck. The bottles were filled upside down, and pressure of the gas in the bottle forced the marble against the washer, sealing in the carbonation. The bottle was pinched into a special shape to provide a chamber into which the marble was pushed to open the bottle. This prevented the marble from blocking the neck as the drink was poured.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> R. White's, by now the biggest soft drinks company in London and south-east England, featured a wide range of drinks on their price list in 1887, all of which were sold in Codd's glass bottles, with choices including strawberry soda, raspberry soda, cherryade and cream soda.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_York_utility_lines_in_1890.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/New_York_utility_lines_in_1890.jpg/170px-New_York_utility_lines_in_1890.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/New_York_utility_lines_in_1890.jpg/255px-New_York_utility_lines_in_1890.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/New_York_utility_lines_in_1890.jpg/340px-New_York_utility_lines_in_1890.jpg 2x" data-file-width="774" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_York_utility_lines_in_1890.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New York in 1890. A street sign "SODA" is visible at the bottom left part of the image</div></div></div>
<p>In 1892, the "<a href="/wiki/Crown_cork" title="Crown cork">Crown Cork Bottle Seal</a>" was patented by <a href="/wiki/William_Painter_(inventor)" title="William Painter (inventor)">William Painter</a>, a Baltimore, Maryland machine shop operator. It was the first bottle top to successfully keep the bubbles in the bottle. In 1899, the first patent was issued for a <a href="/wiki/Glass-blowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass-blowing">glass-blowing</a> machine for the automatic production of glass bottles. Earlier glass bottles had all been hand-blown. Four years later, the new bottle-blowing machine was in operation. It was first operated by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Joseph_Owens" title="Michael Joseph Owens">Michael Owens</a>, an employee of Libby Glass Company. Within a few years, glass bottle production increased from 1,400 bottles a day to about 58,000 bottles a day.
</p><p>In America, <a href="/wiki/Soda_fountain" title="Soda fountain">soda fountains</a> were initially more popular, and many Americans would frequent the soda fountain daily. Beginning in 1806, <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> chemistry professor <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Silliman" title="Benjamin Silliman">Benjamin Silliman</a> sold soda waters in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>. He used a Nooth apparatus to produce his waters. Businessmen in Philadelphia and New York City also began selling soda water in the early 19th century. In the 1830s, <a href="/wiki/John_Matthews_(soda_water_manufacturer)" title="John Matthews (soda water manufacturer)">John Matthews</a> of New York City and John Lippincott of Philadelphia began manufacturing soda fountains. Both men were successful and built large factories for fabricating fountains. Due to problems in the U.S. glass industry, bottled drinks remained a small portion of the market throughout much of the 19th century. (However, they were known in England. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tenant_of_Wildfell_Hall" title="The Tenant of Wildfell Hall">The Tenant of Wildfell Hall</a></i>, published in 1848, the caddish Huntingdon, recovering from months of debauchery, wakes at noon and gulps a bottle of soda-water.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>In the early 20th century, sales of bottled soda increased exponentially around the world, and in the second half of the 20th century, canned soft drinks became an important share of the market. During the 1920s, "Home-Paks" was invented. "Home-Paks" is the familiar <a href="/wiki/Carton" title="Carton">six-pack cartons</a> made from cardboard. <a href="/wiki/Vending_machine" title="Vending machine">Vending machines</a> also began to appear in the 1920s. Since then, soft drink vending machines have become increasingly popular. Both hot and cold drinks are sold in these self-service machines throughout the world.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption">Consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Per capita consumption of soda varies considerably around the world. As of 2014, the top consuming countries per capita were Argentina, the United States, Chile, and Mexico.  Developed countries in Europe and elsewhere in the Americas had considerably lower consumption. Annual average consumption in the United States, at 153.5 liters, was about twice that in the United Kingdom (77.7) or Canada (85.3).<sup id="cite_ref-npr-consumption_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npr-consumption-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent years, soda consumption has generally declined in the West. According to one estimate, per capita consumption in the United States reached its peak in 1998 and has continually fallen since.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A study in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Obesity_(journal)" title="Obesity (journal)">Obesity</a></i> found that from 2003 to 2014 the proportion of Americans who drank a sugary beverage on a given day fell from approximately 62% to 50% for adults, and from 80% to 61% for children.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The decrease has been attributed to, among other factors, an increased awareness of the dangers of obesity, and government efforts to improve diets.
</p><p>At the same time, soda consumption has increased in some low or middle income countries such as <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, India, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> as soda manufacturers increasingly target these markets and consumers have increasing discretionary income.<sup id="cite_ref-npr-consumption_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npr-consumption-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A large green jug" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg/220px-7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg/330px-7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg/440px-7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A jug of bottler's flavor for <a href="/wiki/7_Up" title="7 Up">7-Up.</a> The syrup-like concentrate lacks sugar and is sold to franchisees to refill.</div></div></div>
<p>Soft drinks are made by mixing dry or fresh ingredients with water. Production of soft drinks can be done at factories or at home. Soft drinks can be made at home by mixing a <a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">syrup</a> or dry ingredients with carbonated water, or by Lacto-fermentation. Syrups are commercially sold by companies such as <a href="/wiki/Soda-Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda-Club">Soda-Club</a>; dry ingredients are often sold in pouches, in a style of the popular U.S. drink mix <a href="/wiki/Kool-Aid" title="Kool-Aid">Kool-Aid</a>. Carbonated water is made using a <a href="/wiki/Soda_siphon" title="Soda siphon">soda siphon</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Home_carbonation_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Home carbonation system">home carbonation system</a> or by dropping dry ice into water. Food-grade carbon dioxide, used for carbonating drinks, often comes from <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> plants.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Drinks like <a href="/wiki/Ginger_ale" title="Ginger ale">ginger ale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Root_beer" title="Root beer">root beer</a> are often brewed using <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> to cause <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a>.
</p><p>Of most importance is that the ingredient meets the agreed specification on all major parameters. This is not only the functional parameter (in other words, the level of the major constituent), but the level of impurities, the microbiological status, and physical parameters such as color, particle size, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some soft drinks contain measurable amounts of alcohol. In some older preparations, this resulted from natural fermentation used to build the carbonation. In the United States, soft drinks (as well as other products such as <a href="/wiki/Low-alcohol_beer" title="Low-alcohol beer">non-alcoholic beer</a>) are allowed by law to contain up to 0.5% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a>. Modern drinks introduce carbon dioxide for carbonation, but there is some speculation that alcohol might result from fermentation of sugars in a non-sterile environment. A small amount of alcohol is introduced in some soft drinks where alcohol is used in the preparation of the flavoring extracts such as <a href="/wiki/Vanilla_extract" title="Vanilla extract">vanilla extract</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Market control of the soft drink industry varies on a country-by-country basis. However, <a href="/wiki/PepsiCo" title="PepsiCo">PepsiCo</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">The Coca-Cola Company</a> remain the two largest producers of soft drinks in most regions of the world. In North America, <a href="/wiki/Keurig_Dr_Pepper" title="Keurig Dr Pepper">Keurig Dr Pepper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jones_Soda" title="Jones Soda">Jones Soda</a> also hold a significant amount of market share.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Obesity_and_weight-related_diseases">Obesity and weight-related diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Obesity and weight-related diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1977 to 2002, Americans doubled their consumption of <a href="/wiki/Sweetened_beverage" title="Sweetened beverage">sweetened beverages</a><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>—a trend that was paralleled by doubling the prevalence of obesity.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The consumption of sugar-sweetened beverages is associated with weight and obesity, and changes in consumption can help predict changes in weight.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The consumption of sugar-sweetened soft drinks can also be associated with many weight-related diseases, including diabetes,<sup id="cite_ref-bmj_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bmj-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_syndrome" title="Metabolic syndrome">metabolic syndrome</a>, and cardiovascular risk factors.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dental_decay">Dental decay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dental decay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg/250px-Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="470" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg/375px-Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg/500px-Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="3008" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soft drinks displayed on grocery store shelves.</div></div></div>
<p>Most soft drinks contain high concentrations of simple <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>: <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> and other simple sugars. If oral bacteria ferment <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and produce acids that may dissolve tooth enamel and induce dental decay, then sweetened drinks may increase the risk of <a href="/wiki/Dental_caries" class="mw-redirect" title="Dental caries">dental caries</a>. The risk would be greater if the frequency of consumption is high.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A large number of soda pops are <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a> as are many fruits, sauces, and other foods. Drinking acidic drinks over a long period and continuous sipping may <a href="/wiki/Acid_erosion" title="Acid erosion">erode the tooth enamel</a>. A 2007 study determined that some flavored sparkling waters are as erosive or more so than orange juice.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Using a <a href="/wiki/Drinking_straw" title="Drinking straw">drinking straw</a> is often advised by <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentists</a> as the drink does not come into as much contact with the teeth. It has also been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Oral_hygiene" title="Oral hygiene">brushing teeth</a> right after drinking soft drinks should be avoided as this can result in additional erosion to the teeth due to mechanical action of the toothbrush on weakened enamel.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bone_density_and_bone_loss">Bone density and bone loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bone density and bone loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2006 study of several thousand men and women, found that women who regularly drank cola-based sodas (three or more a day) had significantly lower bone mineral density (BMD) of about 4% in the hip compared to women who did not consume colas.<sup id="cite_ref-tucker_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The study found that the effect of regular consumption of cola sodas was not significant on men's BMD.<sup id="cite_ref-tucker_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benzene">Benzene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Benzene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Benzene_in_soft_drinks" title="Benzene in soft drinks">Benzene in soft drinks</a></div>
<p>In 2006, the United Kingdom <a href="/wiki/Food_Standards_Agency" title="Food Standards Agency">Food Standards Agency</a> published the results of its survey of <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> levels in soft drinks,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> which tested 150 products and found that four contained benzene levels above the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) guidelines for drinking water.
</p><p>The United States <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> released its own test results of several soft drinks containing <a href="/wiki/Benzoates" title="Benzoates">benzoates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Erythorbic_acid" title="Erythorbic acid">erythorbic acid</a>. Five tested drinks contained benzene levels above the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency's</a> recommended standard of 5 <a href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" class="mw-redirect" title="Parts per billion">ppb</a>. As of 2006, the FDA stated its belief that "the levels of benzene found in soft drinks and other beverages to date do not pose a safety concern for consumers".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kidney_stones">Kidney stones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Kidney stones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study published in the <i>Clinical Journal of the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Nephrology" title="American Society of Nephrology">American Society of Nephrology</a></i> in 2013 concluded that consumption of soft drinks was associated with a 23% higher risk of developing <a href="/wiki/Kidney_stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidney stones">kidney stones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_regulation">Government regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Government regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools">Schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since at least 2006, debate on whether high-calorie soft drink <a href="/wiki/Vending_machine" title="Vending machine">vending machines</a> should be allowed in schools has been on the rise. Opponents of the soft drink vending machines believe that soft drinks are a significant contributor to <a href="/wiki/Childhood_obesity" title="Childhood obesity">childhood obesity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tooth_decay" title="Tooth decay">tooth decay</a>, and that allowing soft drink sales in schools encourages children to believe they are safe to consume in moderate to large quantities.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Opponents also argue that schools have a responsibility to look after the health of the children in their care, and that allowing children easy access to soft drinks violates that responsibility.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Vending machine proponents believe that obesity is a complex issue and soft drinks are not the only cause.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> A 2011 bill to tax soft drinks in California failed, with some opposing lawmakers arguing that parents—not the government—should be responsible for children's drink choices.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 3, 2006, the Alliance for a Healthier Generation,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Schweppes" class="mw-redirect" title="Cadbury Schweppes">Cadbury Schweppes</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">The Coca-Cola Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/PepsiCo" title="PepsiCo">PepsiCo</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Beverage_Association" title="American Beverage Association">American Beverage Association</a> announced new guidelines<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> that will voluntarily remove high-calorie soft drinks from all U.S. schools.
</p><p>On May 19, 2006, the British <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Education_and_Skills" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Education and Skills">education secretary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Johnson" title="Alan Johnson">Alan Johnson</a>, announced new minimum nutrition standards for school food. Among a wide range of measures, from September 2006, school lunches will be free from carbonated drinks. Schools will also end the sale of junk food (including carbonated drinks) in vending machines and <a href="/wiki/Tuck_shop" title="Tuck shop">tuck shops</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, Samantha K Graff published an article in the <i>Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science</i> regarding the "First Amendment Implications of Restricting Food and Beverages Marketing in Schools". The article examines a school district's policy regarding limiting the sale and marketing of soda in public schools, and how certain policies can invoke a violation of the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment" class="mw-redirect" title="First Amendment">First Amendment</a>. Due to district budget cuts and loss in state funding, many school districts allow commercial businesses to market and advertise their product (including junk food and soda) to public school students for additional revenue. Junk food and soda companies have acquired exclusive rights to vending machines throughout many public school campuses. Opponents of corporate marketing and advertising on school grounds urge school officials to restrict or limit a corporation's power to promote, market, and sell their product to school students. In the 1970s, the Supreme Court ruled that <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a> was not a form of <a href="/wiki/Free_expression" class="mw-redirect" title="Free expression">free expression</a>, but a form of <a href="/wiki/Business_practices" class="mw-redirect" title="Business practices">business practices</a> which should be regulated by the government. In the 1976 case of <i>Virginia State Board of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> the Supreme Court ruled that advertising, or "<a href="/wiki/Commercial_speech" title="Commercial speech">commercial speech</a>", to some degree is protected under the First Amendment. To avoid a First Amendment challenge by corporations, public schools could create contracts that restrict the sale of certain product and advertising. Public schools can also ban the selling of all food and drink products on campus, while not infringing on a corporation's right to free speech.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 13, 2010, President Obama signed the Healthy Hunger Free Kids Act of 2010<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> (effective in 2014) that mandates schools that receive federal funding must offer healthy snacks and drinks to students. The act bans the selling of soft drinks to students and requires schools to provide healthier options such as water, unflavored low-fat milk, 100% fruit and vegetable drinks or sugar-free carbonated drinks. The portion sizes available to students will be based on age: eight ounces for elementary schools, twelve ounces for middle and high schools. Proponents of the act predict the new mandate it will make it easier for students to make healthy drink choices while at school.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, Terry-McElarth and colleagues published a study in the <i>American Journal of Preventive Medicine</i> on regular soda policies and their effect on school drink availability and student consumption. The purpose of the study was to determine the effectiveness of a program beginning in the 2014–2015 school year that requires schools participating in federally reimbursable meal programs to remove all competitive venues (a la carte cafeteria sales, vending machines, and stores/snack bars/carts), on the availability of unhealthy drinks at schools and student consumption. The study analyzed state- and school district-level policies mandating soda bans and found that state bans were associated with significantly lower school soda availability but district bans showed no significant associations. In addition, no significant correlation was observed between state policies and student consumption. Among student populations, state policy was directly associated with significantly lower school soda availability and indirectly associated with lower student consumption. The same was not observed for other student populations.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxation">Taxation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Taxation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"/><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sugary_drink_tax" title="Sugary drink tax">Sugary drink tax</a></div>
<p>In the United States, legislators, health experts and consumer advocates are considering levying higher <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a> on the sale of soft drinks and other sweetened products to help curb the epidemic of <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> among Americans, and its harmful impact on overall health. Some speculate that higher taxes could help reduce soda consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Duffey2010_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duffey2010-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Others say that taxes should help fund education to increase consumer awareness of the unhealthy effects of excessive soft drink consumption, and also help cover costs of caring for conditions resulting from overconsumption.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drink_industry" title="Drink industry">drink industry</a> holds considerable clout in Washington, DC, as it has contributed more than $50 million to legislators since 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2013, a British <a href="/wiki/Lobby_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Lobby group">lobby group</a> called for the price of sugary fizzy drinks to be increased, with the money raised (an estimated £1 billion at 20p per litre) to be put towards a "Children's Future Fund", overseen by an independent body, which would encourage children to eat healthily in school.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates and the Kingdom of Bahrain imposed a 50% tax on soft drinks and a 100% tax on energy drinks to curb excess consumption of the commodity and for additional revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bans">Bans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Bans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2013, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>'s mayor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a> proposed to ban the sale of non-diet soft drinks larger than 16 ounces, except in convenience stores and supermarkets. A lawsuit against the ban was upheld by a state judge, who voiced concerns that the ban was "fraught with arbitrary and capricious consequences". Bloomberg announced that he would be appealing the verdict.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> The state appellate courts upheld the trial court decision, and the ban remains unenforceable as of 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
